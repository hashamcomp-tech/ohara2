Chapter 688

Kaela’s laugh came out wrong, half hysteria, half disbelief.

Maurien didn’t even look at her.

“Don’t lose focus,” he snapped, voice like ice hammered into shape.

Kaela’s eyes flashed, but her hands didn’t drop. Wind tightened around the hull again as another wave slammed into them and the ship lurched.

Renvar grunted, sweat and rain indistinguishable on his face. “I’m—trying—” he forced out, teeth clenched.

Somehow, together, they held it.

They had been forced to, there was no room for choice when the ocean tried to flip them like a toy. Their wind barrier had been there just in time, a fraction of a second before total catastrophe.

But even as the ship steadied and the barrier’s shimmer began to thin with exhaustion, the truth settled over the deck heavier than the rain:

They had survived this time. By inches. By timing. By three mages burning themselves to keep wood from becoming a coffin.

And they wouldn’t be that lucky the next time. The wind barrier held until the ship finished righting itself.

Then it let go.

Not because Kaela, Maurien, and Renvar wanted to release it, because their bodies finally reached the point where holding it another heartbeat would mean their hands stopped obeying.

The pressure around the hull dispersed in a violent exhale. Air snapped outward in a ring, rain blown sideways, spray ripped off the deck. For a split second, it felt like the ship had been freed from a giant fist.

And those three didn’t waste it. Kaela’s eyes flashed and she shoved with everything she had left. Maurien joined it with a cold, precise push. Renvar snarled and poured his last stable gust into the same direction. The combined burst hit the sails and the air behind the ship like a battering ram.

The

S.S. Elaine surged.

The bow rose sharply, sudden acceleration lifting the front like the ship was trying to climb out of the sea entirely. Men stumbled. Ropes snapped taut. Water sheeted off the hull in a waterfall as the ship shot forward.

Then the bow settled back down, cutting into the waves again, speed still gained, still carrying them away from where the monster had flipped them. For a few breaths, it felt like escape.

Everyone was breathing rough now, faces pale under lantern light. Sailors clung to rails with knuckles white, eyes wide and glassy, whispering prayers they hadn’t believed in until tonight. Viola stood braced near the side, drenched, jaw clenched, scanning the black water like she could force it to stay away. Shera stared into the storm with wild focus, hands trembling. Valk stayed grounded, but even he looked tense now, less serene monk, more man ready to die correctly.

Kaela hunched over, hands on her knees, chest heaving. Renvar looked like he might vomit. Maurien’s face was carved from exhausted concentration. They all stared forward as if staring hard enough could make the sea decide to stop hunting them.

They wanted this to be the end. Then it happened.

The waterspouts, the four towering cyclones that had climbed toward the clouds, began to move.

At first it was subtle. A slow drift. A change in their alignment. Then they started to slide toward the ship with purpose. And as they moved, they grew.

Their funnels widened, sucking more water into their rotation, dragging the sea into spinning ridges. The surface around them dipped and rose in jagged patterns, turning the water into a hungry, twisted maze.

The ship’s speed faltered. The wind caught wrong. Foam and spray surged sideways as if the ocean itself was trying to pull them back.

The

S.S. Elaine shuddered, slowed, then lurched as the current tugged at her hull.

Like the spouts were ropes. Like the sea had grown hands again. Kaela lifted her head, stared at the approaching towers of spinning water, and let out a tired, bitter sigh.

“…I guess that is it,” she said, voice thin with exhaustion. “It was fun while it lasted.”

The words hit the deck like a curse.

Ludger stood nearby, soaked, shaking from backlash, his circuits still burning, his mana pool scraped clean enough that his senses felt hollow. He could feel how empty he was, like someone had scooped out his power and left only heat and pain.

But he wasn’t ready to give up. Not while people were still breathing.

He stared at the waterspouts, mind racing through options he didn’t have the mana to execute. Harpoons? Useless in this weather. Turtle Shock Wave? Not with his circuits screaming and his pool dry. Earth? On a ship? In a storm? Maybe, if…

He didn’t get to finish the thought. Because everyone was looking forward. Everyone’s attention was locked on the approaching cyclones. And that’s when the monster hit them from the other side.

A shadow rose.

A massive tail exploded out of the black water with no warning, no buildup, no visible approach in the rain, just violence arriving at their flank like an executioner’s blade. It slammed into the ship.

The impact snapped the world sideways. Bodies flew.

People smashed into rails and posts, backs hitting wood with sickening thuds. Grunts of pain broke out everywhere as breath got knocked out of chests. A sailor cried out and collapsed, arms wrapped around his ribs.

Viola slammed hard into the rail, teeth gritted, eyes wide in shock.

Shera went down on one knee, catching herself with a hand just before her face met the deck. Valk absorbed the impact with a deep stance, still sliding a half-step before he locked himself in place.

And then someone went over.

A figure hit the rail wrong, with her head, too hard, then slipped through a breach where the wood had cracked earlier.

There was a sharp, hollow knock, a head hitting the rail. And the body vanished into the storm-dark sea.

Luna. Viola saw it. Her face went white in an instant.

“Luna!” she screamed.

The name tore out of her raw, desperate, too loud to be anything but truth. She didn’t think. She didn’t hesitate. She jumped. Straight for the rail, straight into the rain, straight toward the place her best friend had disappeared.

Ludger moved on instinct. No mana. No time. Just body. He launched after her, caught her by the back of her coat in mid-air, and yanked. Viola jerked violently as Ludger’s grip stopped her momentum.

For a split second, their eyes met, Viola’s wide with terror, Ludger’s hard with decision.

He didn’t have mana to save them. Not with shields. Not with wind.

Not with anything clever. So he did it the only way he could. He threw her back onto the deck.

Viola hit the planks hard, sliding, hands scrambling for purchase, alive, shocked, screaming Luna’s name again as if the sound could pull her back up.

Ludger didn’t land.

He fell in the ocean after jumping after Viola and grabbing her in the air…

He disappeared into the ocean like a stone, swallowed immediately by black water and storm foam.

The last thing visible was the brief flash of lightning catching his silhouette mid-fall… and then he was gone too.

It happened so fast it felt wrong. The moment Luna vanished beneath the black water… the moment Ludger followed her down… the storm began to die. Not slowly, like weather usually did.

It lost power.

The wind weakened first, its screaming edge fading into something almost normal. The rain thinned from needles into scattered drops, then into mist. The waves, those towering walls, settled into heavy swells, still dangerous but no longer impossible.

And the waterspouts…

They unraveled.

Their funnels stuttered, thinned, and then collapsed as if someone had cut the strings holding them upright. Columns of spinning water fell back into the sea in ragged curtains, foam exploding outward as the rotations died.

The ocean stopped trying to drag the ship into their mouths.

The monster, whatever massive presence had been toying with them, didn’t strike again.

No tail. No lift. No final blow. Just darkness and the sound of water, suddenly ordinary. For a long, stunned stretch, no one moved.

Men clung to rails and ropes with knuckles still white, breathing hard, eyes wide, staring into the night like they expected the storm to remember it was supposed to be killing them.

Kaela stood hunched over, chest rising and falling, face pale, then slowly lifted her head as if she didn’t trust what her senses were telling her.

Maurien’s gaze flicked across the sky, then the sea, then the places the cyclones had been. His expression shifted from combat focus to something colder.

Confusion.

Rathen stared at the water with his mouth slightly open, hands still locked on the wheel as if letting go would make reality snap back into the storm again. Viola lay on the deck for a heartbeat longer, drenched, shaking, the world muffled by shock.

Then it hit her. Not the ship. Not the storm. The absence. Her eyes snapped open wider, and she pushed herself up hard enough to scrape skin on wet wood.

“They…” her voice cracked. She swallowed, forced air into her lungs. “They’re gone.”

No one answered. Because the sea had just taken two people… and then gone calm, like it was satisfied.

Viola’s head turned sharply, eyes wild.

“It’s like—” she started, then stopped, unable to say it without giving it power.

Like the sea wanted sacrifices. And it got them. That thought hung over the deck like a curse. Viola’s hands curled into fists until her knuckles went white. Then, finally, her voice found steel again.

“We have to look for them,” she said, louder now, the tone of someone who refused to accept the ocean’s terms. “Ludger and Luna. We have to.”

Rathen flinched as if the words physically hit him.

He hesitated. The captain’s instincts screamed that turning around in deep water after a night like this was a good way to join the dead. His crew stared at him, waiting for the decision that decided whether they were sailors… or survivors.

After a long, brutal second, Rathen nodded.

“Turn about,” he rasped.

He swallowed, eyes haunted. “We search.”

He gave the orders, voice rough, and the ship slowly swung, cutting a cautious arc back through the calmer, almost mocking—sea.

The night dragged on after that in a numb blur. Everyone searched.

Sailors lined the rails with lanterns, sweeping the water in slow arcs, calling out when they thought they saw movement, only to fall silent again when it turned out to be foam or debris.

Kaela and Maurien used wind magic to probe the surface.

Not a flashy spell, something practical: gusts that pushed foam aside, currents that rippled outward to expose anything floating, air patterns that might catch a scent or a sign.

Kaela’s hands shook from exhaustion, but she still worked until her shoulders quivered. Maurien’s jaw stayed clenched, eyes sharp, every gust controlled and searching.

Nothing. No body. No bubble trail. No answer. Shera stood near the rail, face tight, scanning with unnatural focus, eyes searching for mana traces the way she searched for creature signatures.

Nothing.

Viola stayed at the rail for hours, rain drying off her skin as the storm fully retreated, staring until her eyes burned. Every time the ship circled, she leaned forward as if sheer will could make the ocean cough them up.

The night refused.

Only when morning finally came, when the first gray light spread across the horizon and the sun began to paint the waves in dull gold, did the sea reveal anything at all.

A sailor shouted first.

“Something!” he cried, pointing.

Heads snapped. Kaela pushed wind across the surface, clearing foam. And there it was. A piece of cloth drifting on the water like a small, surrendered flag. A green scarf.

Ludger’s scarf.

The one he wore like it didn’t matter, like it was just fabric, but everyone knew it mattered anyway. Viola’s breath caught so hard it sounded like she’d been punched.

She didn’t wait for permission.

She grabbed a rope, tied it around her waist, and leaned over the rail as far as she could without falling. Hands reached to steady her. Someone hooked the scarf with a pole and guided it close.

Viola snatched it up with shaking fingers.

The cloth was soaked, heavy, cold. She stared at it for a heartbeat, as if expecting it to change into him. Then her face crumpled. The sound she made wasn’t a scream. It was quieter than that. A broken, choking noise that came from somewhere deep and raw.

She pulled the scarf to her chest and hugged it, fingers crushing the fabric as if holding it tighter could stop the world from taking more.

Her shoulders began to shake.

Tears fell, silent at first, then harder, spilling down her cheeks and into the scarf, mixing with saltwater and morning mist.

Viola bowed her head over it, clutching the cloth like it was the last piece of them she was allowed to keep.

And as the sunlight brightened the deck and everyone stood around her in stunned, helpless silence… there were still no signs of Ludger.

No signs of Luna.

Only the quiet sea, calm as a liar, and a scarf floating like the ocean’s idea of mercy.
